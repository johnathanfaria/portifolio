7.1 Setting a function to remove outliers¶

In [8]:
def remove_outliers(df,variable):
  
    Q1= df[variable].quantile(0.25)
    Q3= df[variable].quantile(0.75)
    IQR= Q3-Q1
    lower_bound= Q1 - 1.5 * IQR
    upper_bound= Q3 + 1.5 * IQR
    
   #outliers = df[(df[variable] < lower_bound) | (df[variable] > upper_bound)]
    new_cleanData=df[(df[variable] >=lower_bound) & (df[variable] <=upper_bound)]

    return new_cleanData

8. Describing the results seen in the boxplots¶

  1. Some users who experienced a high number of call failures did not churn, which is surprising. According to the boxplot, users who churned had, in median, around 6 call failures.

  2. Regarding subscription length, churn occurrences were most commonly observed (median) after approximately 35 months. However, some users with shorter subscription durations also churned (outliers under the bottom whisker, even after removing them). The reason for those new outliers is explained in the next item.

  3. When it comes to time of use, the median usage time for churned users was around 1,000 seconds. In addition, it's possible to observe that the more a customer uses the service, the less likely they are to churn (as also seen in the heatmap below).

  4. The exception to this correlation was found in the frequency of SMS usage. A few users who sent a large number of text messages still churned.

  5. It’s also evident that higher customer value is associated with lower churn likelihood. The median customer value among churned users was around 100.

Heatmap of the correlation of the Variables of interest¶

Heatmap Takeaways.¶

  1. As expected, variables that measure time of use and frequency of use are highly positively correlated with each other.

  2. Confirming the boxplot analysis above, there is no strong indication of a correlation between call failures and churn (correlation coefficient = 0.091). While a higher number of call failures could lead to user dissatisfaction and potentially increase the likelihood of churn, this pattern is not clearly supported by the data.

  3. Customer value is positively correlated with all usage-related variables, except for churn, where the correlation is negative — which is an expected outcome. Extremely high positive correlations (values >0.7) are observed with variables measuring frequency and time of use. The negative correlation with churn suggests that the company's customer value metric effectively captures user engagement and is a good predictor of churn likelihood.

Kurtosis' analysis takeaways¶

  1. Almost all variables showed a negative delta kurtosis, except for Seconds of Use. This suggests that the outlier removal was effective in reducing extreme values.

  2. The largest drop was observed in the Distinct Called Numbers variable, with a 125% reduction. The resulting negative kurtosis indicates very few remaining outliers (as seen in the boxplot in item 7.2).

  3. However, three variables still showed high kurtosis values even after outlier removal. This explains the large number of outliers that were still visible in their boxplots, despite the data amputation:

  • Seconds of Use (kurtosis = 1.94)

  • Frequency of SMS (kurtosis = 1.68)

  • Customer Value (kurtosis = 0.97)
